## Power System
The power system of *Death Note* is a "hard" supernatural system governed by a strict, bureaucratic set of laws known as the **"How to Use"** rules. Unlike battle shonen systems (like Nen or Haki), progression is measured by **information and strategy** rather than physical strength.

### **1. Core Mechanics & Abilities**
The system revolves around the **Death Note**, a notebook from the Shinigami Realm.
*   **The Kill Condition:** To kill a human, the user must write their name while picturing their face. This prevents people with the same name from being affected.
*   **The Default:** If only a name is written, the victim dies of a **heart attack** in **40 seconds**.
*   **Cause & Details:**
    *   **40-Second Window:** After writing a name, the user has 40 seconds to specify a cause of death.
    *   **6-Minute 40-Second Window:** After the cause is written, the user has an additional 6 minutes and 40 seconds to write specific details/actions leading up to the death.
*   **Mind Manipulation:** The notebook can control a victim's actions before they die, provided the actions are physically and mentally possible for that individual. If a condition is impossible (e.g., "fly to the moon"), the victim simply dies of a heart attack.

### **2. Rules & Limitations**
The system is defined more by its constraints than its power.
*   **The 23-Day Rule:** A death can only be scheduled up to 23 days in advance unless it involves a disease that naturally takes longer to kill.
*   **Age Limits:** The notebook does not affect humans under **780 days old** (approx. 2 years) or over **124 years old**.
*   **The 12-Minute Rule:** You cannot kill someone who has less than 12 minutes of "natural" life remaining.
*   **Misspelling:** If a name is misspelled four times accidentally, that person becomes immune to that specific Death Note. If the user misspells a name four times *intentionally* to grant immunity, the user dies.
*   **Six-Notebook Limit:** Only six Death Notes can exist in the human world at one time.

### **3. Tiers & Hierarchy**
The power system is divided into three distinct tiers of authority and existence:

| Tier | Entity | Power/Role |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tier 1** | **Shinigami King** | The source of all notebooks and rules. He can create new laws at will (e.g., the 2020 rule forbidding the sale of the notebook). He is "the rules" personified. |
| **Tier 2** | **Shinigami** | Ranked 1–13 (lower numbers are higher rank). They are functionally immortal as long as they kill humans to steal their remaining lifespan. They possess "Shinigami Eyes" by default. |
| **Tier 3** | **Human Users** | Mortals who find a notebook. They cannot extend their own lives; they can only shorten them or use the notebook to manipulate the world. |

*   **Shinigami Ranking:** While a numerical rank exists (e.g., Rem is Rank 4, Ryuk is Rank 6), it rarely affects their daily life. It is primarily a measure of their "stats" (Intelligence, Curiosity, Proactiveness) and their standing with the King.

### **4. Progression & Power-Ups**
There is no "leveling up" in *Death Note*, but there are two forms of progression:

*   **The Shinigami Eye Deal:** This is the only "active" power-up. A human can trade **half of their remaining lifespan** to gain the ability to see any human's name and lifespan just by looking at their face.
*   **Strategic Mastery:** Progression is represented by the user's ability to exploit loopholes. 
    *   *Example:* Light Yagami "progresses" by discovering he can use the notebook to store memories. By relinquishing ownership and then regaining it, he can execute complex "memory-wipe" gambits to bypass investigations.
*   **Ownership Cycles:** A human can regain their memories of the notebook up to **six times**. After the sixth time, they can no longer regain their memories even if they touch the notebook again.

### **5. Limitations on Shinigami**
Shinigami are not omnipotent; they are bound by "Shinigami Law":
*   **The Sacrifice Rule:** If a Shinigami uses their notebook to intentionally extend a human's life (by killing someone who was about to kill that human), the Shinigami **dies instantly** and turns to dust. Their remaining lifespan is transferred to the human they saved.
*   **The 9-Meter Rule:** A Shinigami must stay within roughly 9 meters of the human owner of their notebook unless they are in the Shinigami Realm.

## Combat
In *Death Note*, "combat" is redefined from physical brawls into a high-stakes psychological and tactical warfare. While traditional action is rare, the series uses cinematic spectacle and intellectual "fighting styles" to make mental duels feel like epic battles.

### 1. Physical Combat & Fighting Styles
Though infrequent, physical confrontations serve as a mirror to the characters' internal philosophies.
*   **L’s Style (Capoeira-inspired):** L is a surprisingly capable fighter. During his handcuffed brawl with Light, he utilizes a style heavily inspired by **Capoeira**, characterized by "spring-loaded" kicks and fluid, defensive movement. This reflects his personality: grounded, balanced, and reactive.
*   **Light’s Style (Emotional Aggression):** Light fights with raw, unrefined aggression, primarily using his fists. This symbolizes his "God complex"—a desire to strike down and control others from above.
*   **Mello’s Style (Violent Pragmatism):** Unlike the others, Mello engages in traditional "combat" through his mafia connections. He uses explosives, firearms, and kidnapping, representing a more impulsive and physically dangerous tactical approach.

### 2. Intellectual "Fighting Styles"
The characters approach mental battles with distinct tactical identities:
*   **The Proactive Aggressor (Light):** Light’s style is offensive. He takes massive risks to eliminate threats (e.g., the Lind L. Tailor broadcast or the Naomi Misora encounter). He uses the Death Note as a long-range "one-hit kill" weapon, relying on deception and social engineering to get close enough to "strike."
*   **The Reactive Counter-Puncher (L):** L fights by setting traps and waiting for Light to overextend. His "combat" is defensive; he uses logic to narrow down the "hitbox" of his opponent until Kira has nowhere to hide.
*   **The Analytical Strategist (Near):** Near’s style is purely detached and mathematical. He treats the battle like a puzzle, refusing to engage emotionally and instead focusing on overwhelming his opponent with a "checkmate" through sheer evidence.

### 3. Tactical Depth & Maneuvers
Battles are won through specific tactical "moves" that function like special abilities:
*   **The Alibi Maneuver (Potato Chip Scene):** Light uses a mundane object (a chip bag) as a tactical blind spot. He hides a mini-LCD TV and a scrap of the Death Note inside to kill criminals while under 24/7 surveillance, turning a snack into a high-tech weapon.
*   **The Decoy Strike (Lind L. Tailor):** L uses a death-row inmate as a "human shield" to bait Light into revealing his location. This is the intellectual equivalent of a "feint" in a sword fight.
*   **Emotional Manipulation:** Light’s "finishing move" against Naomi Misora is not a physical strike but an emotional one. He exploits her grief to lower her guard, eventually "executing" her by tricking her into revealing her real name.

### 4. Flashy Spectacle & Cinematic Battles
The anime uses "spectacle" to make non-physical scenes feel explosive:
*   **Visual Color Coding:** During intense mental duels, the world fades to black, and characters are bathed in signature lighting—**Red for Light** (aggression/evil) and **Blue for L** (calm/logic). This creates a "flashy" visual language for internal thoughts.
*   **Dramatic Directing:** Director Tetsurō Araki uses aggressive camera pans, Dutch angles, and a booming orchestral/gospel score (e.g., *Low of Solipsism*) to elevate writing in a notebook to the level of a high-speed action sequence.
*   **The Tennis Match:** This is the series' most literal "spectacle battle." Every serve and volley is framed as a mental exchange, with the score of the game reflecting the shifting advantage in their investigation.

### 5. Emotional Weight
The "combat" in *Death Note* is fueled by intense ego and desperation.
*   **The God Complex:** Light’s emotional state shifts from calculated genius to frantic megalomania when cornered, making his "battles" feel like a descent into madness.
*   **The Cost of Victory:** Battles often end in "mutual destruction" or heavy emotional tolls, such as the death of Light's father or L’s eventual fall, ensuring that every tactical win feels heavy and permanent.

## Tone
*Death Note* is a seminal psychological thriller known for its high-stakes intellectual battles and philosophical depth. Its tone is a complex blend of dark realism, supernatural horror, and occasional absurdist humor.

### **1. Genre and Mood**
*   **Genre:** Primarily a **psychological thriller**, **mystery**, and **supernatural fiction**. It also functions as a **tragedy**, charting the moral collapse of its protagonist.
*   **Mood:** The mood is consistently **tense and cerebral**. It evokes a sense of "cat-and-mouse" suspense where every conversation is a tactical move. The series often feels claustrophobic, focusing on the internal monologues of geniuses trapped in a battle of wits.

### **2. Tone: Dark Realism vs. Melodrama**
The tone of *Death Note* is famously "edgy" but sophisticated. It balances two distinct styles:
*   **Gothic Grandeur:** The series uses religious iconography (crosses, apples, church bells) and a dramatic, operatic score to give Light’s "divine mission" a sense of epic importance.
*   **Clinical Coldness:** Despite the supernatural premise, the world is portrayed as secular and indifferent. The Shinigami (Death Gods) are not agents of fate but bored observers, making the human struggle feel both monumental and ultimately meaningless.

### **3. Themes**
*   **Justice vs. Morality:** The central conflict explores whether a "good" end (a crime-free world) justifies "evil" means (murder). It pits Light’s **utilitarianism** (the greatest good for the greatest number) against L’s **deontological** view of law and order.
*   **Power and Corruption:** The series is a case study in how absolute power corrupts. Light begins with arguably noble intentions but quickly becomes a narcissist who kills anyone—criminal or innocent—who threatens his ego.
*   **The Nature of Evil:** It questions if "evil" is an inherent trait or a result of circumstance. When Light loses his memories of the notebook, he reverts to a kind, moral person, suggesting the Death Note itself acts as a catalyst for his latent darkness.

### **4. Dark Comedy**
While predominantly serious, *Death Note* is famous for its **absurdist dark humor**, often stemming from the contrast between the life-and-death stakes and the characters' eccentricities:
*   **Ryuk’s Apple Addiction:** The terrifying God of Death becomes a comic relief figure due to his physical withdrawal symptoms (contorting his body and doing handstands) when deprived of apples.
*   **L’s Eccentricities:** L’s monotone delivery, strange sitting positions, and obsession with sweets provide a dry, observational humor that undercuts the tension.
*   **Over-the-Top Drama:** The anime adaptation is known for its "dramatic mundane" moments—most famously the **"Potato Chip Scene,"** where Light uses cinematic slow-motion and an epic orchestral score to perform the simple act of eating a chip while secretly writing names.

### **5. Optimism (The "Twisted" Kind)**
Optimism in *Death Note* is rare and usually subverted:
*   **Light’s Utopia:** Light is a "twisted optimist." He genuinely believes he can create a perfect world. However, the series treats this as a delusion of grandeur rather than a hopeful message.
*   **Human Resilience:** A more traditional sense of optimism is found in the **Task Force**. Characters like Soichiro Yagami represent the "incorruptible" human spirit—people who believe in justice and the law even when faced with a god-like killer.
*   **The Ending:** Some interpret the ending as optimistic because it restores the natural order, proving that no individual is "above" humanity. Others see it as cynical, as the world simply returns to its previous state of "boring" crime and apathy.

### **6. Atmosphere and Visual Style**
*   **Visuals:** The atmosphere is built through a **"classy" but bleak** aesthetic. The anime uses a desaturated color palette (heavy on blues, greys, and deep reds) and dramatic lighting to emphasize shadows.
*   **Music:** The soundtrack, composed by Yoshihisa Hirano and Hideki Taniuchi, is vital to the atmosphere. It ranges from **Gothic/Neo-Baroque** (choirs and pipe organs for Light) to **ambient/minimalist** (electronic and "detective-style" guitar for L). This duality mirrors the clashing ideologies of the two leads.

## Characters
*Death Note* is a psychological thriller that centers on a high-stakes battle of wits between a brilliant student who gains the power of a god and the elite detectives trying to stop him.

### **Core Characters: Roles & Abilities**

| Character | Role | Primary Abilities |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Light Yagami (Kira)** | **Protagonist / Villain** | Genius-level intellect, master manipulator, and user of the **Death Note** (can kill anyone by writing their name while picturing their face). |
| **L Lawliet** | **Main Antagonist / Hero** | World’s greatest detective. Exceptional deductive reasoning, vast global resources, and a "childish" competitive nature that refuses to lose. |
| **Misa Amane** | **Supporting Protagonist** | Pop idol and "Second Kira." Possesses the **Shinigami Eyes**, allowing her to see any person's real name and remaining lifespan just by looking at them. |
| **Ryuk** | **Neutral Observer** | A Shinigami (Death God) who dropped the notebook out of boredom. He is invisible to those who haven't touched his notebook and can see names/lifespans. |
| **Near (Nate River)** | **Secondary Antagonist** | L’s primary successor. He is cold, analytical, and uses toys/puzzles to visualize his theories. He eventually leads the SPK to catch Kira. |
| **Mello (Mihael Keehl)** | **Secondary Antagonist** | L’s second successor. Unlike Near, he is impulsive and emotional. He uses criminal connections (the Mafia) to hunt Kira through force and intimidation. |

---

### **The Protagonist vs. Antagonist Dynamic**
*Death Note* is unique because the **protagonist (Light Yagami)** is the story's villain. 
*   **Light Yagami:** Driven by a "God complex," he seeks to create a utopia by executing criminals. While he is the character the audience follows, his actions are morally reprehensible.
*   **L Lawliet:** Though he is the **antagonist** (because he opposes the protagonist), he represents the law and traditional justice. The conflict is a "cat-and-mouse" game where both characters are constantly trying to uncover the other's identity without revealing their own.

---

### **Key Relationships**

#### **Light and L: The Rivalry**
Their relationship is defined by mutual respect and intense suspicion. L is the only person Light considers an intellectual equal, and L admits that Light is the "first friend" he has ever had—though this was likely a tactical lie to lower Light's guard. Their battle is purely mental, fought through psychological traps and social maneuvers.

#### **Light and Misa: The Manipulation**
Misa is obsessively in love with Light because he (as Kira) killed the murderer of her parents. Light does not love Misa; he views her strictly as a tool because her **Shinigami Eyes** can reveal L’s true name. He frequently manipulates her devotion to protect himself, while Misa is willing to sacrifice her own life for his goals.

#### **Light and Ryuk: The Boredom Bond**
Ryuk is not Light’s ally. He follows Light only for entertainment and apples. He explicitly tells Light that when his time comes, Ryuk will be the one to write Light's name in his own notebook. Their relationship is one of "predator and observer."

#### **Near and Mello: The Successors**
Near and Mello grew up in Wammy’s House (an orphanage for gifted children) as rivals for the title of "L." Individually, neither could surpass L, but together their contrasting styles—Near’s logic and Mello’s action—eventually cornered Light. Near famously states that they only succeeded because they worked toward the same goal from different angles.

#### **Rem and Misa: The Protector**
Unlike the indifferent Ryuk, the Shinigami **Rem** genuinely loves Misa. This maternal bond becomes a weakness that Light exploits; he forces Rem into a situation where she must kill L to save Misa’s life, which results in Rem’s own death (as Shinigami die if they intentionally extend a human's life).

## Factions
In the world of *Death Note*, the conflict is driven by a complex web of law enforcement agencies, secret organizations, and criminal syndicates. The factions are broadly divided between those supporting Kira’s "New World" and those seeking to uphold the traditional rule of law.

### 1. The Kira Faction (The "New World")
This group consists of individuals directly assisting Light Yagami or acting as his proxies to carry out his vision of a crime-free world.
*   **Light Yagami (Kira):** The central figure and "God of the New World." He operates from within the Japanese police to sabotage investigations.
*   **Misa Amane (The Second Kira):** A devoted follower who uses her "Shinigami Eyes" to assist Light. She provides a second Death Note and acts as a public distraction.
*   **Teru Mikami (X-Kira):** A fanatical prosecutor chosen by Light to act as his proxy when Light is under heavy surveillance. He is more ruthless than Light, often killing those with criminal records even if they have been rehabilitated.
*   **Kiyomi Takada:** Light’s college girlfriend and a news anchor who becomes Kira’s official spokesperson. She serves as the communication bridge between Light and Mikami.

### 2. The Anti-Kira Factions
These groups are dedicated to identifying and arresting Kira, though they often clash due to differing methods and leadership.
*   **The Kira Investigation Team (Japanese Task Force):** 
    *   **Leadership:** Initially led by **Soichiro Yagami** (Light's father) and the detective **L**. After L's death, Light takes over as the "New L."
    *   **Members:** Includes dedicated officers like Aizawa, Matsuda, Mogi, and Ide. 
    *   **Role:** They represent the moral core of the series, struggling with the dilemma of working with a "God" (Light) while suspecting he might be the killer.
*   **The SPK (Special Provision for Kira):**
    *   **Leadership:** Led by **Near (Nate River)**, L’s primary successor.
    *   **Composition:** An elite group of FBI and CIA agents (notably Anthony Rester, Stephen Gevanni, and Halle Lidner) funded by the U.S. government.
    *   **Role:** They operate independently of the Japanese police and are more clinical and technologically advanced in their pursuit of Kira.
*   **Wammy’s House:** 
    *   An orphanage in Winchester, England, founded by **Watari**. It serves as a training ground for "genius" children intended to succeed L. Key graduates include L, Near, Mello, and Matt.

### 3. Third-Party & Opportunistic Factions
These groups use the Death Note or the Kira phenomenon for personal gain, often acting as "wild cards."
*   **The Mafia (Mello’s Group):**
    *   **Leadership:** Led by **Rod Ross**, but effectively directed by **Mello (Mihael Keehl)**.
    *   **Role:** Mello joins the Mafia to hunt Kira through criminal means, using kidnapping (Sayu Yagami) and violence to obtain a Death Note. They are enemies of both Kira and the SPK.
*   **The Yotsuba Group:**
    *   **Composition:** Eight high-ranking executives of the Yotsuba Corporation.
    *   **Role:** During a period when Light loses his memories, one of them (**Kyosuke Higuchi**) uses the Death Note to kill corporate rivals, turning Kira into a tool for financial profit.

### 4. World Powers & Geopolitics
The existence of Kira shifts the global balance of power, forcing nations to choose between resistance and submission.
*   **The United States:** Initially supports the SPK, but as Kira’s power grows, the U.S. President (**David Hoope**) eventually surrenders to Kira’s authority to avoid being killed. By the end of the series, the U.S. officially recognizes Kira as "Justice."
*   **Japan:** The primary battleground. The National Police Agency (NPA) is torn between its duty to catch a murderer and the reality that Kira has reduced the national crime rate by over 70%.
*   **Interpol:** Early in the series, Interpol organizes a global summit to address the "Kira phenomenon," leading to the recruitment of L.

### 5. Key Alliances and Rivalries
*   **L vs. Kira:** The defining rivalry of the first half. It is a battle of pure intellect and "justice" vs. "morality."
*   **Near vs. Mello:** A rivalry between L's successors. Near represents L’s cold logic, while Mello represents L’s action-oriented, impulsive side. Their inability to work together initially helps Kira, but their eventual (indirect) cooperation leads to Light's downfall.
*   **Kira vs. The World:** By the series' end, Kira has effectively "won" geopolitically, with most world leaders ceasing their opposition. His only remaining enemies are the small, underground remnants of the SPK and the Task Force.

### 6. Kira’s "Kingdom" (Public Supporters)
*   **Sakura TV:** A sensationalist TV station led by **Hitoshi Demegawa** that broadcasts Kira’s messages and hosts "Kira’s Kingdom," a show that deifies him.
*   **Kira Cults:** General members of the public who worship Kira as a god. They are shown in the series' epilogue as a lingering religious movement even after Light’s death.

## Locations
The setting of *Death Note* is primarily grounded in modern-day Japan, specifically within the **Kanto region**, but its narrative reaches across the globe to the United Kingdom and the United States, as well as into the supernatural **Shinigami Realm**.

### **1. Primary Setting: Japan (Kanto Region)**
The majority of the series takes place in and around **Tokyo**. The geography is highly realistic, often featuring real-life landmarks.

*   **The Yagami Household:** Located in a quiet residential area of Tokyo. The most critical spot is **Light’s Bedroom**, which he rigged with surveillance-thwarting measures and a desk containing a hidden compartment with a self-destruct (incendiary) device to protect the Death Note.
*   **Daikoku Private Academy:** The high school Light attends at the start of the series. It is depicted as an elite institution, reflecting Light's status as a top student.
*   **To-Oh University:** The prestigious university where Light and L (as Hideki Ryuga) enroll. It is widely considered to be based on the real-life **University of Tokyo** or **Aoyama Gakuin University**.
*   **Japanese Task Force Headquarters:** 
    *   **Initial Locations:** L initially operates out of various high-end hotels, such as the **Imperial Hotel** in Tokyo, to maintain anonymity.
    *   **The Task Force Building:** Later, L commissions a custom 23-story skyscraper in Tokyo. It features extreme security, including fingerprint and retina scans, and serves as the base of operations for the investigation until L's death.
*   **Sakura TV:** A fictional television station (likely based on **Fuji TV** or **Sakura TV** in real life) located in Tokyo. It is the primary media outlet used by the "Second Kira" (Misa Amane) and later by the Kira spokesperson, Hitoshi Demegawa.
*   **Yellow Box Warehouse (YB Warehouse):** Located on **Daikoku Wharf in Yokohama**. This is the site of the series' final confrontation between Light Yagami and Near. It is a large, yellowish-brick industrial building with a single entrance, symbolizing Light's "cornered" state at the end of the story.

### **2. International Locations**
The "Kira Case" is a global phenomenon, leading to several key international settings.

*   **Wammy’s House (Winchester, England):** An orphanage for gifted children established by Quillsh Wammy (Watari). Its secret purpose was to raise a successor to L. This is the origin of **Near, Mello, and Matt**.
*   **United States (Washington D.C. & New York):**
    *   **FBI Headquarters:** The base for the 12 agents sent to Japan to investigate the Japanese police.
    *   **SPK Headquarters:** The **Special Provision for Kira** (led by Near) operates out of a secret, high-tech facility in the U.S. In the anime, Light uses the U.S. President to leak its location, leading to a siege by Kira supporters.
*   **The Mafia Hideout:** During the second half of the series, Mello operates from various secret locations in the U.S., including an underground base where he holds Sayu Yagami hostage.

### **3. The Supernatural Realm**
*   **The Shinigami Realm:** A desolate, barren dimension that exists "above" the human world. 
    *   **Geography:** It is characterized by dry earth, bone-like structures, and vast deserts. There are no buildings, only strange rock formations and chains.
    *   **Observation Holes:** The realm contains "holes" or "spheres" through which Shinigami can look down into the human world to choose victims.
    *   **The Shinigami King’s Cave:** A central area where the ruler of the Shinigami resides, often surrounded by hanging chains and hooks.

### **4. Real-Life "Pilgrimage" Sites**
Fans often visit real locations in Tokyo that inspired the series:
*   **Shinjuku Station:** Where Light met and manipulated FBI agent Raye Penber.
*   **Hibiya Park:** The location of the tense walk between Light and Naomi Misora.
*   **Shibuya Crossing:** Frequently shown in establishing shots to emphasize the scale of Kira’s influence over the masses.
*   **Aoyama Cemetery:** A recurring atmospheric backdrop for various meetings and transitions.

## Arcs, Sequels, Adaptations, Recent
*Death Note*, created by Tsugumi Ohba and Takeshi Obata, is one of the most influential psychological thrillers in manga and anime history. Below is a comprehensive breakdown of its story arcs, sequels, adaptations, and current status.

### **1. Major Story Arcs & Plot Progression**
The series is generally divided into two "Parts" across 12 manga volumes (108 chapters) or 37 anime episodes.

*   **The L Arc (Part I: Chapters 1–59 / Episodes 1–25):**
    *   **Kira vs. L:** High schooler Light Yagami finds the Death Note and begins killing criminals as "Kira." The world's greatest detective, L, narrows Light's location to the Kanto region of Japan via a televised trap.
    *   **The Second Kira:** Misa Amane, an idol with "Shinigami Eyes," seeks out Light. Her recklessness forces Light into a corner, leading him to voluntarily enter confinement to "prove" his innocence by relinquishing ownership of the notebook.
    *   **Yotsuba Arc:** With his memories erased, Light joins L to catch the "Third Kira" (Kyosuke Higuchi), who is using the book for corporate gain. Upon touching the book again, Light regains his memories and kills Higuchi.
    *   **L’s Death:** Light manipulates the Shinigami Rem into killing L and his assistant Watari to save Misa. Light then assumes the mantle of "L" to lead the investigation against himself.

*   **The Near & Mello Arc (Part II: Chapters 60–108 / Episodes 26–37):**
    *   **The Successors:** Five years later, L’s successors, Near (SPK) and Mello (Mafia), begin separate investigations. Mello kidnaps Light’s sister, Sayu, forcing a confrontation that leads to the death of Light’s father, Soichiro.
    *   **The Final Showdown:** Near eventually deduces Light is Kira. They meet at the Yellowbox Warehouse. Light’s proxy, Teru Mikami, is caught using a fake notebook, exposing Light.
    *   **The End:** In the manga, a desperate Light begs Ryuk to kill the investigators; instead, Ryuk writes Light's name. In the anime, Light flees the warehouse and dies alone on a staircase.

---

### **2. Sequels, Spinoffs, & Continuity**
The *Death Note* franchise has several distinct continuities:

*   **Manga Continuity (The "Canon" Timeline):**
    *   **Death Note Another Note: The Los Angeles BB Murder Cases:** A prequel novel following L and Naomi Misora as they hunt a serial killer (Beyond Birthday) before the Kira case.
    *   **C-Kira One-Shot (2008):** Set 3 years after Light's death. A new Kira emerges who kills the elderly to "relieve their pain." Near (now L) dismisses him as a "Cheap Kira" (C-Kira), and the user eventually commits suicide.
    *   **a-Kira One-Shot (2020):** Set 10 years after Light's death. A student named Minoru Tanaka auctions the Death Note to world leaders (including a fictionalized Donald Trump) rather than using it to kill.

*   **Japanese Live-Action Continuity:**
    *   **Death Note (2006) & The Last Name:** These films follow the manga but feature a massive ending change: **L survives Light** by writing his own name in a Death Note first, ensuring he dies peacefully 23 days later after catching Kira.
    *   **L: Change the World (2008):** A spinoff focusing on L's final 23 days.
    *   **Light Up the NEW World (2016):** A sequel set 10 years later where six Death Notes are dropped on Earth simultaneously.

### **4. Latest Updates & Current Events (2024–2026)**
The franchise remains active through new media and anniversary projects:

*   **Netflix Live-Action Series (Duffer Brothers):** Announced in 2022, the creators of *Stranger Things* are developing a new live-action series through their production company, Upside Down Pictures. As of **early 2026**, the project is still in active development but has been slow due to the Duffers' focus on the final season of *Stranger Things*. It is expected to be a "reboot" rather than a sequel to the 2017 film.
*   **Death Note: Killer Within (2024):** A social deduction game (similar to *Among Us*) was released by Bandai Namco, where players take on roles as Kira, L, or investigators to find the notebook or eliminate rivals.
*   **20th Anniversary (2026):** With the anime's 20th anniversary in 2026, various merchandise collaborations and "Relight" screenings have been revived in Japan and on streaming platforms like Netflix.
*   **The "Shinigami Light" Theory:** Fans still debate the *Death Note Relight* (OVA) movies, which feature an unnamed Shinigami asking Ryuk about the human world. Many believe this Shinigami is a reincarnation of Light Yagami due to his clothing and limp.
